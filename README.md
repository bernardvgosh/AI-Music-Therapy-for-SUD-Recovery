## `01` &nbsp; The Problem Space

People in recovery from substance use disorder face a landscape where professional treatment is essential but often insufficient on its own — access is limited, support between sessions is sparse, and the emotional terrain of recovery is relentless. Two distinct bodies of work offer potential tools to fill that gap:

- **Music-based interventions** — from clinical music therapy to structured listening — carry decades of evidence on emotional regulation, craving response, and therapeutic engagement.
- **Artificial intelligence** — from affect-detection systems to conversational agents and lapse-prediction models — offers scalability, personalisation, and around-the-clock availability that no human clinician can match.

Neither literature has adequately spoken to the other. This project maps both, surfaces their intersections and contradictions, and proposes an integrative framework.

## `02` &nbsp; The Four-Pillar Framework

The study is organised around four functions these interventions might serve in recovery. The pillars are analytically distinct but empirically entangled.

<br/>

<div align="center">

| # | Pillar | Core Question | Key Tension |
|---|--------|--------------|-------------|
| **I** | **Emotional Regulation** | Can music and AI-enabled affect tools help people recognise, tolerate, and modulate emotional states without recourse to substance use? | Affect-detection systems claim more certainty than their validation supports |
| **II** | **Craving Reduction** | What is the evidence that these tools attenuate the urge to use — and when does music *induce* craving instead? | The **craving-cue paradox**: music is a conditioned associative stimulus, not a unidirectional therapeutic tool |
| **III** | **Self-Expression** | How do songwriting, lyric analysis, improvisation, and AI co-creation support identity reconstruction in recovery? | Generative AI co-creation tools are largely unvalidated in clinical populations |
| **IV** | **Personalised Recovery Support** | How can AI tailor content, timing, and modality to the individual over time — and what are the limits of doing so? | Privacy, equity, and the risk of over-reliance during crisis are highest here |

</div>

<br/>

> **Design constraint — the craving-cue paradox.** A systematic review by Silverman and colleagues (2023) *(citation under verification)* found that music can induce craving for substances including alcohol, cannabis, and nicotine by acting as a contextual cue. This is not a caveat to be noted and set aside — it is a structural constraint that runs through all four pillars.

```
  for people with substance use disorder?
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  RQ1 │ EMOTIONAL REGULATION
      │ What is the evidence that music-based interventions and AI-enabled
      │ affective tools improve emotional regulation in people with SUD,
      │ and through what proposed mechanisms?

  RQ2 │ CRAVING
      │ What is the evidence that these tools reduce craving — and under
      │ what conditions might music instead act as a craving cue?

  RQ3 │ SELF-EXPRESSION
      │ How do music-based therapeutic techniques and AI co-creation tools
      │ support self-expression and identity reconstruction in recovery?

  RQ4 │ PERSONALISATION
      │ How can AI personalise recovery support across time, and what are
      │ the limits and risks of doing so?

  RQ5 │ ETHICS (cross-cutting)
      │ What ethical, methodological, and equity considerations should govern
      │ the responsible development and study of these tools?
```

## `04` &nbsp; Methodological Design

| Dimension | Decision | Rationale |
|-----------|----------|-----------|
| **Review type** | PRISMA-ScR Scoping Review | Maps a heterogeneous, emerging, cross-disciplinary field; more honest fit than systematic review with narrow criteria |
| **Synthesis approach** | Separate-literatures synthesis | The AI × music × SUD intersection is expected to be thin; insisting on it would produce an underpowered review |
| **Population anchor** | Alcohol use disorder (AUD) | AUD carries the densest AI literature; broader SUD evidence admitted where AUD-specific work is absent, always flagged |
| **Output** | Proposed conceptual framework | The integrative framework, not empirical overlap, is the original contribution |
| **Evidence labelling** | Established / Emerging / Speculative | Applied to every key finding — this is not optional decoration |

> These three scope decisions are **locked** and should not be reopened. Each was made for defensible reasons; reversing any mid-stream is the most reliable path to timeline collapse.

## `05` &nbsp; Evidence Orientation

The following characterises where the two literatures stand. **Treat every claim here as a starting hypothesis** — each must be verified against primary sources before entering submitted material.

### Music-Based Interventions

The most authoritative synthesis is the Cochrane review by **Ghetti et al. (2022)** — 21 randomised trials (~1,984 participants), reporting moderate-certainty evidence of a medium-sized effect favouring music therapy added to standard care over standard care alone for substance craving. This is the single strongest evidence point in the field.

Earlier reviews (Hohmann et al., 2017; Megranahan & Lynskey, 2018) found beneficial effects on emotional and motivational outcomes, but with substantial inconsistency, heavy reliance on single-session designs, and a near-absence of longitudinal trials.

**Honest summary:** *Promising and improving, but heterogeneous and immature.*

### AI in SUD Treatment & Recovery

The applied AI literature is younger and more fragmented — weighted toward feasibility studies, protocols, and narrative reviews rather than confirmatory trials. Active areas include ML lapse-prediction from EMA and wearable data (Heinz et al., 2025), conversational agents for around-the-clock support (with mixed safety evaluations — Giorgi et al., 2024), and JITAIs pairing ML predictions with personalised support messages.

The most useful framing is from **Moniz-Lewis et al. (2025)** in *Alcohol Research: Current Reviews*: real promise for equitable, scalable, compassionate care — alongside serious and enumerated ethical concerns.

**Honest summary:** *Real and accelerating activity, genuine promise, but very few rigorously validated tools deployed in clinical settings.*

## `06` &nbsp; Ethical Framework

Ethics is not a closing chapter. The following five concerns are **design constraints** that shape questions and methods from the start, and belong next to the claims they qualify — not quarantined to a final section.

<br/>

```
  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  1. ADJUNCT FRAMING                                             │
  │     State plainly and repeatedly: these tools supplement        │
  │     professional, evidence-based treatment. Nothing in this     │
  │     work encourages replacing clinical care with an app.        │
  ├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │  2. THE CRAVING-CUE PARADOX                                     │
  │     Music can trigger as well as soothe craving. Any music-     │
  │     based or music-recommending tool carries a specific,        │
  │     foreseeable risk of harm. Explicit treatment required.      │
  ├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │  3. DATA SENSITIVITY & PRIVACY                                  │
  │     Recovery data — location, physiological signals, craving    │
  │     logs — is among the most sensitive personal data there is.  │
  │     Its disclosure can carry legal and social consequences for  │
  │     an already-stigmatised group.                               │
  ├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │  4. EQUITY OF ACCESS                                            │
  │     Tools that depend on smartphones, wearables, connectivity,  │
  │     and digital literacy can widen rather than narrow           │
  │     disparities. Ask consistently: who is served, who excluded? │
  ├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │  5. RISK OF OVER-RELIANCE                                       │
  │     Affect-detection systems present more certainty than they   │
  │     possess. A conversational agent during a crisis is a        │
  │     genuine safety concern. Escalation pathways and clinical    │
  │     validation are requirements, not features.                  │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## `10` &nbsp; Language & Standards

This project applies the following non-negotiable standards throughout:

- **Person-first language** — "people with a substance use disorder," never "addicts" or "users"
- **Terminological precision** — music therapy (MT) ≠ music-based intervention (MBI) ≠ consumer music listening; these distinctions are maintained every time they matter
- **Evidence calibration** — *established* (consistent RCT/meta-analytic support) · *emerging* (early trials, feasibility) · *speculative* (proposed mechanism, no direct SUD evidence)
- **Verify before asserting** — no empirical claim reproduced from memory or summary; all checked against primary sources
- **Ethics integrated** — adjunct framing, craving-cue paradox, privacy, equity, and over-reliance concerns appear next to the claims they qualify, not in a closing chapter
